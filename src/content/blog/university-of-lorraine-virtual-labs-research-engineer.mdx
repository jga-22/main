Some professional experiences teach you how to build systems.

Others teach you how people learn.

My experience as a **research engineer at the University of Lorraine** gave me both.

I worked on **Action 1.4 of the DUNE EOLE project**, an initiative focused on using **virtual labs in the factory of the future**. At the center of the work was a question that I still find powerful today: how can we use digital tools not only to simulate industrial systems, but also to transform the way engineering is taught?

This was not only a technical project.

It was also a pedagogical one.

And that is what made it so meaningful to me.

## Working at the intersection of engineering and education

The project sat at the crossroads of several worlds that usually do not meet so naturally:

- industrial engineering
- digital twins
- simulation
- virtual reality
- collaborative robotics
- pedagogy
- project-based learning

The ambition was clear: develop **virtual training scenarios** around key Industry 4.0 themes and connect them to real physical resources at the university.

The work relied on two flexible manufacturing workshops and two collaborative robotics stations hosted by the University of Lorraine. Their **virtual twins** were designed to support immersive and more accessible learning experiences. Instead of limiting learning to physical access to the equipment, the project aimed to create a richer environment where students could prepare, explore, simulate, and then connect concepts back to real installations.

For me, that idea was extremely inspiring.

It suggested that digital technology in education should not only reproduce existing teaching formats. It should expand what is possible.

## More than content creation

At first glance, a project like this can sound like content development.

But in reality, it was much broader than that.

The goal of Action 1.4 was to develop **five virtual training scenarios** from Bac+2 to Bac+5, centered on major themes of the factory of the future:

- industrial automation
- production line management and supervision
- industrial robotics
- collaborative robotics
- industrial maintenance

Those themes reflected the real complexity of modern industry. They also required more than slides or standard practical sessions. To make them meaningful, the learning experience had to become more visual, more interactive, and more connected to real systems.

That is where digital twins and 3D simulation became so valuable.

## A different vision of practical learning

One of the things I appreciated most in this experience was the pedagogical model behind it.

The project proposed a **flipped lab** logic. Instead of discovering everything for the first time on the physical equipment, students could first access digital learning spaces, explore the virtual twins of the systems, and understand the concepts through immersive tools before applying them on real installations.

This approach changed the role of the student.

The student was no longer just receiving information. The student became more active in the learning process through:

- project-based learning
- flipped-classroom dynamics
- learning by doing
- immersive learning

That shift matters a lot to me.

Because I have always believed that deep learning happens when people are not only told what a system is, but when they can interact with it, experiment with it, and build meaning through action.

## What I actually did

In that context, my role was not limited to one narrow technical task.

I contributed to the operational development of the action, helping turn pedagogical ambition into usable deliverables. That included supporting the development of content, demonstrators, training materials, dashboards, and learning pathways across the different themes.

What I find especially meaningful when I look back is the scale of what was produced.

According to the activity report, the project resulted in around 50 deliverables, including 8 virtual labs, 5 virtual learning dashboards, 5 learning platforms, 21 identified practical sessions with pedagogical objectives, 5 learning pathways, and 4 pedagogical guides. Beyond development, the work also included teacher training, interventions in courses, and support for pedagogical projects.

That was a lot more than building a demo.

It was about building an ecosystem.

## A collaborative way of building

Another important part of the experience was the way the project was organized.

The development was carried out with a broad network of pedagogical and technical contributors from different parts of the university, working together with DITEX and with support from Dassault Systèmes. I liked this very much because it reflected something I still value in large or ambitious projects: meaningful outcomes usually come from the combination of **knowledge** and **know-how**.

The academic side brings the learning objectives, the scientific perspective, and the pedagogical intent.

The engineering side brings implementation, structure, tooling, and operational execution.

When these two dimensions work well together, something much more valuable can emerge.

## Why this experience stayed with me

This experience had a lasting impact on me because it expanded the way I thought about digital transformation.

In industry, we often talk about digital twins, simulation, or smart systems in terms of performance, optimization, and operations.

At the University of Lorraine, I saw another side of that story.

I saw how those same ideas could become tools for **transmission**, **accessibility**, and **learning**.
> A virtual twin was no longer only an industrial asset. It also became a pedagogical asset.  
> A simulation was no longer only for design validation. It could also be used to structure understanding.
> A dashboard was no longer only for monitoring. It could also become a learning space.

That was an important shift in perspective for me.

## Building for adoption, not only for innovation

Another lesson that stayed with me was that creating innovative educational content is only one part of the challenge.

The project was also concerned with **adoption**, **diffusion**, and **continuity**. Once development was completed, the next challenge was ensuring that the teaching community would adopt the new materials, diffuse them across programs, and continue developing new virtual practical sessions over time.

I find that lesson universal.

Whether you are building for education, industry, or digital transformation, success is rarely just about producing something technically impressive.

It is about making sure it can live, spread, and be used by others.

That requires training, documentation, communication, and a clear path for others to appropriate the work.

In that sense, this experience also taught me something about scalability.

Not scalability in the cloud sense.

Scalability in the human sense.

## Why it still matters to me today

When I look back, I do not only remember a project about virtual labs.

I remember an experience that connected several themes that still shape the way I think today:

- digital twins as operational and learning tools
- human-centered design in technical environments
- the value of immersive and active learning
- the importance of translating complexity into usable experiences
- the role of collaboration between education, research, and industry

This experience helped me understand that technology becomes far more powerful when it is used not only to automate or optimize, but also to **enable learning**.

That idea still resonates with me strongly.

Because at the end of the day, the future of engineering is not only about more advanced systems.

It is also about helping more people understand, use, and shape those systems.

And this project was a beautiful example of that.
