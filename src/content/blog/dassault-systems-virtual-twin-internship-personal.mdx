Some experiences stay with you, not only because of what you built, but because of how they changed the way you see technology.

My internship at **Dassault Systèmes** was one of those experiences.

At the time, I was working on a project around **Poppy**, an open-source humanoid robot created at Inria, in collaboration with **ENSAM** and Dassault Systèmes. The ambition was both technical and educational: to explore how the **3DEXPERIENCE platform** could help create a **virtual twin** of a robotic system, and how that virtual twin could support design, control, simulation, and interaction.

Today, words like *digital twin* and *virtual twin* are everywhere. But back then, for me, this was not a buzzword. It was the first time I could really see what it meant to create a meaningful bridge between the **physical world** and the **digital world**.

And that idea stayed with me.

## Discovering that a model can become operational

Before that experience, like many engineering students, I saw models mostly as tools for analysis, design, or validation.

This project pushed me to think differently.

The virtual twin was not only there to represent the robot. It became part of the way we reasoned about movement, interaction, and system behavior. It helped connect what the robot **was** with what it **could do**.

That shift was important for me.

I started to understand that a digital model is most powerful when it is not treated as static documentation, but as something alive — something that can support decision-making, control, learning, and communication between systems and people.

## A project at the intersection of many disciplines

What made the internship so rich was that it did not sit in only one technical domain. It was not “just robotics,” and it was not “just simulation.”

It combined several dimensions of engineering that I still find deeply exciting today:

- **robot control**
- **computer vision**
- **human-machine interaction**
- **co-simulation between software environments**
- **systems thinking across physical and digital layers**

The project itself was structured around two scenarios.

The first, **Poppy Parallel**, focused on human-robot interaction, with different robot applications launched from a user interface.

## Working on control, perception, interaction, and simulation

One part of the project focused on the **control system**. We worked on moving a point of the robot's hand to different positions using the virtual twin to obtain inverse kinematics. A safety mechanism was also integrated to detect collisions and preserve the integrity of both the robot and the user.

Another important part was **artificial vision**. We developed a color-based object recognition approach that enabled behaviors such as following an object with the head and even using the detected object's movement to influence the position of the robot's arm.

This may sound simple compared with today's AI-heavy robotics landscape, but that is precisely why it was such a valuable experience. It taught me that elegant engineering is not always about using the most complex method. Sometimes, it is about finding the right level of intelligence for the objective and integrating it well into the whole system.

We also worked on a **human-robot interface**, which allowed users to launch applications and monitor variables linked to actuator performance. That part mattered more than it might seem. It reminded me that advanced technology becomes truly meaningful when it is accessible, observable, and usable by humans.

And finally, there was the **co-simulation** dimension, using the **FMI (Functional Mock-up Interface)** concept to establish a connection between the 3DEXPERIENCE platform and the robot's actuators through Python tools such as **Pypot**. This was one of the most formative aspects of the project for me, because it showed how much value can be created when different tools are made to cooperate instead of remaining isolated.

## What I really learned

Looking back, the technical work was only part of the value of that internship.

What I really learned was something broader.

I learned that the future of engineering is not only about designing components. It is about designing **relationships**:

- between real systems and virtual systems
- between simulation and execution
- between algorithms and interfaces
- between technical rigor and human usability

That experience also strengthened my interest in systems that are both **concrete and conceptual**. A robot is a physical object. But once you connect it to models, interfaces, control logic, and simulation environments, it becomes something much larger: a system of systems.

I think that internship helped shape my long-term attraction to topics like **digital continuity**, **industrial transformation**, **AI systems**, and **architecture**. Even though my work has evolved since then, I can still see the continuity.

In many ways, that internship gave me an early glimpse of the questions that still drive me today:

How do we connect tools instead of fragmenting them?  
How do we make models useful beyond documentation?  
How do we design technology that is technically strong, but also operationally meaningful?

## Why it still matters to me today

When I look back, I do not only remember a robotics internship.

I remember one of the first moments when engineering felt bigger than code, bigger than mechanics, and bigger than software taken separately.

It felt like a way of thinking.

A way of building bridges between worlds that are too often disconnected.

And maybe that is why this experience still resonates with me today. It was an early encounter with a theme that continues to shape my work: the idea that technology creates the most value when it connects disciplines, systems, and people into something coherent.

That is what this internship represented for me.

Not only a project on a humanoid robot, but an introduction to a much larger vision of engineering.
