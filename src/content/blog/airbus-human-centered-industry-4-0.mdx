Some experiences shape the way you think long after the project itself is over.

My time at **Airbus Nantes** was one of those experiences.

During that period, I worked on digital projects in an industrial environment and wrote my professional thesis on a question that still feels very relevant to me today:

> **How can human-centered approaches contribute to the success of technological projects in the context of Industry 4.0?**

At first sight, Industry 4.0 is often presented through technology: connected tools, data, AI, IoT, augmented reality, digital twins, smarter interfaces, better traceability, and more automation.

But what I progressively realized at Airbus is that technology is rarely the hardest part.

The real challenge is often elsewhere: **adoption, understanding, alignment, and usability**.

## Looking beyond the technology itself

My thesis was built around a simple conviction: **technology is not the goal of transformation, but the means**.

The real objective is to improve operational performance, collaboration, working conditions, and value creation. In other words, digital transformation only matters if it creates something meaningful for the business and for the people who are part of it.

That is what made the topic of **human-centered design** so important to me.

Industry 4.0 is not only a technological shift. It is also a strategic, organizational, cultural, and human transformation. It changes how people work, how they learn, how they collaborate, and how they interact with tools.

That is why I wanted to explore the place of the human in the middle of all this.

## Airbus Nantes as a real industrial context

What made this work especially valuable is that it was grounded in a real industrial setting.

At Airbus Nantes, the context was complex, demanding, and deeply operational. The site brought together thousands of collaborators, multiple production lines, several product types, and an industrial ecosystem connected to larger Airbus programs. The digital ambition was real, and so was the pressure to make transformation useful on the ground.

That context taught me something essential: in industry, a digital solution is never only about the solution itself. It also has to fit the rhythm of operations, the constraints of production, the diversity of users, and the practical reality of the shop floor.

This is where many transformation efforts become difficult.

## The question that stayed with me

As part of the work, I looked at both broader Industry 4.0 literature and the local Airbus Nantes context.

One of the strongest ideas that emerged was this: the success of industrial transformation depends less on the existence of technologies than on the ability of people to **adopt**, **understand**, and **value** them.

That may sound obvious, but it is often underestimated.

In many organizations, the discussion around transformation focuses on tools, budgets, platforms, or roadmaps. Those things matter. But they do not automatically create adoption.

A tool can be technically solid and still fail in practice if users do not see the value, do not feel involved, or do not find it usable in their daily work.

That gap became central in my reflection.

## What the fieldwork revealed

One of the most interesting parts of the experience was the fieldwork.

The thesis combined external research with local surveys and interviews carried out at Airbus Nantes. What I found especially striking was the difference between how contributors and users perceived the integration of end users in digital projects.

In the presentation summarizing the work, only **20% of users** felt that their needs were well taken into account in the development of new tools, while **80% of contributors** believed they were already integrating final users during the design phase.

That difference says a lot.

It suggests that the issue is not always bad intention or lack of effort. Sometimes, teams genuinely believe they are being user-centered, while users still experience the outcome very differently.

Other findings pointed in the same direction: users wanted stronger support around training, clearer interfaces, and better integration into the design of new tools, even when operational constraints made that difficult.

For me, this was one of the most important lessons of the whole experience: **there is often a significant gap between designing for users and designing with users**.

## Why human-centered approaches mattered

This is where the thesis moved from diagnosis to proposal.

I explored how **human-centered approaches** could help reduce that gap and improve the success of industrial digital projects. The core idea was not to import design methods in a superficial way, but to adapt them intelligently to an industrial context.

The thesis looked at several complementary approaches:

- **Design Thinking**, to better understand users, contexts, frustrations, and expectations
- **Lean Startup**, to test hypotheses early and learn before scaling
- **Agile/Scrum**, to iterate and deliver progressively instead of waiting too long for a “perfect” solution

What I liked about this combination is that it made transformation feel less abstract.

Instead of saying “we should be more user-centric,” it offered a more operational path:
understand people better, test earlier, learn faster, improve continuously, and involve users throughout the journey.

That logic felt very powerful to me then, and it still does now.

## A recommendation shaped by reality

One of the recommendations of the work was to integrate these human-centered principles into Airbus's project environment, particularly by aligning them with the existing innovation and project logic rather than treating them as a parallel process.

That mattered a lot.

Because in large industrial organizations, good ideas do not survive on theory alone. They need to connect with governance, constraints, timing, investment logic, operational pressure, and the reality of how projects are actually delivered.

So the challenge was never simply “apply design thinking.”

The challenge was: **how do you adapt human-centered principles to an industrial system that is already structured, constrained, and moving at scale?**

That is a question I still find fascinating today.

## What this experience changed in me

Looking back, this experience influenced me far beyond the thesis itself.

It reinforced my belief that the best transformations are not the ones that deploy the most technology. They are the ones that create coherence between:

- strategy and execution
- tools and users
- innovation and operations
- digital ambition and human reality

It also deepened my interest in topics that still drive me today: digital transformation, industrial systems, enterprise architecture, product thinking, and the design of solutions that people can truly adopt.

In many ways, Airbus helped me see that meaningful innovation is not only about building new systems. It is about building systems that people can understand, trust, and use.

## Why this still matters to me today

When I look at the work I am interested in now, whether in AI, industrial systems, product design, or enterprise transformation, I can clearly see the continuity.

This Airbus experience taught me that solving the technical problem is only part of the job.

The deeper challenge is to create solutions that fit into real contexts, respect real constraints, and help real people do their work better.

That is probably why this experience still resonates with me.

It was not only a thesis about Industry 4.0.

It was an early lesson in something broader: **technology creates the most value when it is designed around people, not only around possibilities**.
