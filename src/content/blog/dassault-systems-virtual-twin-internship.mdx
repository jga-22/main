During my internship at Dassault Systèmes, I had the opportunity to work on a project that sat at the intersection of robotics, simulation, and digital engineering. Looking back, it was one of the experiences that most deeply shaped the way I think about technology today.

The project focused on **Poppy**, an open-source humanoid robot originally created at Inria. In collaboration with **ENSAM** and **Dassault Systèmes**, the goal was to explore how the **3DEXPERIENCE platform** could be used to design and operate a **virtual twin** of a robotic system.

At the time, the term *digital twin* was far less mainstream than it is today. But even then, the core idea was already powerful: create a meaningful link between the **real system** and its **virtual counterpart** during design, programming, and use, so that both worlds can inform each other.

## Working on a robot through its virtual twin

What made this experience especially interesting was that it was not just about simulation for demonstration purposes. The virtual model was part of the logic of the system itself.

The project was structured around two main scenarios:

- **Poppy Parallel**, focused on human-robot interaction and the ability to launch different robot applications from a user interface.
- **Poppy Compliant**, focused on taking advantage of the virtual twin for robotic control.

This gave the work a strong practical direction. The challenge was not only to model a robot, but to make the virtual layer useful for control, interaction, and experimentation.

## Four development axes

The project evolved through four main areas of development, each one contributing to the broader vision of a usable virtual twin.

### 1. Control system

One part of the work focused on controlling a point on the robot's hand so it could move to different positions. This relied on the virtual twin to compute the robot's **inverse kinematics**.

A safety mechanism was also integrated to detect collisions with objects, helping protect both the robot and its users. This was an early lesson for me: intelligent systems are not only about capability, but also about constraints, robustness, and safe interaction with the physical world.

### 2. Artificial vision

Another major axis was **computer vision**. We developed a color-based object recognition approach that became the foundation for two functionalities:

- **Poppy Follower**, where the robot tracks an object with its head and keeps it centered in its field of view.
- **Poppy Follower of Gestures**, where the robot's right arm follows the movements of the detected object.

This was a concrete example of how perception and motion can be connected in a simple but meaningful way. It also showed me how even relatively accessible vision techniques can unlock engaging robotic behaviors when they are integrated well.

### 3. Human-robot interface

We also developed a **human-robot interface** to make the system more interactive and easier to use. Through this interface, users could launch multiple robot applications and visualize variables related to actuator performance.

That part of the work mattered a lot. A technical system only becomes truly useful when people can understand it, interact with it, and observe what it is doing. Even at that stage of my journey, I was already drawn not only to the engineering itself, but also to the question of how to make advanced systems usable.

### 4. Co-simulation

The fourth axis was **co-simulation**, using the **FMI (Functional Mock-up Interface)** concept to connect simulation and design software.

This made it possible to establish a link between the **3DEXPERIENCE platform** and the robot's actuators, which communicated through the **Pypot** Python library. A tutorial was also proposed to illustrate how control techniques developed in **Matlab/Simulink** could be integrated into the 3DEXPERIENCE environment through co-simulation.

For me, this was one of the most formative dimensions of the project. It highlighted a principle that still matters in modern engineering systems: value often comes not from a single tool, but from the ability to make tools cooperate.

## What this internship taught me

This experience left a lasting impression on me for several reasons.

First, it gave me early exposure to the idea that **virtual representations can become operational assets**, not just design artifacts. That idea now sits at the center of many modern approaches to digital continuity, simulation-driven engineering, and industrial AI.

Second, it showed me the power of working across disciplines. Robotics is not only mechanics, control, or software. It becomes most interesting when those domains converge with interface design, simulation, and real user interaction.

Third, it reinforced something I still believe strongly today: technology becomes more meaningful when it is used to **connect worlds** that are too often separated — physical and digital, human and machine, model and execution, concept and application.

## Why it still matters to me

When I look at my current interests in AI, industrial systems, architecture, and digital transformation, I can trace many of them back to experiences like this one.

Working on robotics and virtual twins at Dassault Systèmes was not just a technical internship. It was one of the first times I experienced what it means to build systems that are both **engineered** and **conceptual**: systems that require technical rigor, but also a broader vision of how technologies interact and create value together.

It was an early chapter, but an important one.
